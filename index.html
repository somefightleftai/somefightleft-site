What I Do

The Why

The fight was never with the other guy.

I trained karate for about 27 years under one of Hidetaka Nishiyama's senior students — and roughly a decade directly under Sensei Nishiyama himself, until he passed in 2008. Funakoshi to Nishiyama to me, hand to hand. I didn't read the old teachings. I lived them on the floor.

The same year I started karate, I started yoga — Iyengar for the first twenty years, then Ashtanga; now the primary series, most mornings. Most people go the other way, softening with age. I did it in reverse, like usual. Iyengar called it involution — turning inward to grow. Karate got me there through resistance, yoga through surrender. Same door, two ways in.

Now, the last five years, I'm the student again — learning John Danaher's jiu-jitsu at 62. Deep in some arts. A beginner in another. That's the point. You never arrive.

The true opponent is yourself.
Master yourself before you try to master anyone else. That's why age doesn't retire you — the fight with your own limits never ends.
Give up control to get control.
Sensei Nishiyama's paradox. The harder you grip, the faster you lose it. Let go — let the other guy move — and the control comes to you. On the mat. In the pose. In life.
You bow to thank them.
The opponent is a mirror. The resistance is the teacher. You bow because being tested is a gift.
The Real Fight

The fight goes first.

Lose it and you won't even notice. That's the danger.

I've always been a do-it-my-way guy. Ran my own companies. Spent the last ten years inside a big one — and I watched what it does to people. Not the work. The fight. It smooths you out. Rewards going along. Turns you into a cheerleader for mediocrity until the part of you that pushed back just goes quiet. And most people never feel it leave.

That's the real fight — not the one on the mat. The one to stay awake. To keep generating instead of coasting. To not become someone performing a life he used to actually live.

I stopped reading the philosophy two decades ago because I was living in other people's heads. Made my own body and mind the experiment instead. Sensei told me: I give you good food — you must digest it yourself. So I did. Still do. 62, still a student, still figuring it out, still fighting for it.

Keep your fight. It's not a metaphor — it's what's keeping you alive. Some fight left, baby.
